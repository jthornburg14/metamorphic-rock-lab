Lab Introduction

Metamorphic rocks are rocks that have been changed in the solid state by heat, pressure, and/or hydrothermal fluids — without melting. They record some of the most extreme conditions in Earth's crust and are fundamental to understanding mountain building, subduction, and the rock cycle.

In this lab you will examine hand specimens and images to identify metamorphic rocks, determine their texture and mineralogy, assess their metamorphic grade, and infer their protolith (parent rock).

What Is Metamorphism?

Metamorphism occurs when pre-existing rock (the protolith) is subjected to conditions — heat, pressure, or chemically active fluids — that cause solid-state mineralogical and textural changes. The rock never melts; if it did, the process would be igneous, not metamorphic.

The Three Agents of Metamorphism

AgentSourceEffect
HeatMagmatic intrusions, geothermal gradient, frictionDrives recrystallization and new mineral growth
PressureBurial (confining) or tectonic (directed/differential)Compacts rock; directed stress causes mineral alignment (foliation)
Hydrothermal FluidsMagma-derived or circulating groundwaterTransport ions, enable chemical reactions, cause metasomatism

Types of Metamorphic Change

ProcessDescriptionResult
RecrystallizationExisting minerals grow larger without changing compositionCoarser grain size; e.g., limestone → marble
NeocrystallizationNew minerals form from original mineral assemblageNew index minerals appear (garnet, staurolite)
ReorientationPlaty minerals rotate perpendicular to directed stressFoliation — parallel alignment of minerals
Pressure SolutionMinerals dissolve under stress and reprecipitate elsewhereGrain flattening; stylolites

Metamorphic Environments

EnvironmentConditionsTypical Rocks
Contact MetamorphismHigh heat only; adjacent to igneous intrusions; low to moderate pressureHornfels, marble, quartzite
Regional MetamorphismHeat + pressure over large areas; burial or convergent tectonicsSlate, phyllite, schist, gneiss
Dynamic MetamorphismHigh pressure, low temperature; fault zonesMylonite
Shock MetamorphismExtreme instantaneous pressure; meteorite impactShocked quartz, coesite

Classifying Metamorphic Rocks

The first step in identifying a metamorphic rock is always texture: does it show foliation (planar mineral alignment) or not?

Foliated
Minerals arranged into parallel planar layers. Results from directed (differential) stress causing platy minerals to align. Grain size and the type of foliation increase with metamorphic grade.

Examples: Slate, Phyllite, Schist, Gneiss
Non-Foliated
No planar mineral alignment. Texture is granular or massive. Typically forms when the rock is composed of minerals that are not platy (e.g., quartz, calcite) or formed under uniform pressure (contact metamorphism).

Examples: Quartzite, Marble, Hornfels, Amphibolite

Identification Decision Tree

Step-by-step metamorphic rock identification
Step 1: Is the rock foliated (planar mineral alignment visible)? ▼
YES → The rock has a layered or banded texture. Proceed to Step 2 to determine the type of foliation.
NO → The rock lacks foliation. Proceed to Step 4 to classify by composition.
Step 2 (Foliated): Can you see individual mineral grains? ▼
NO — very fine-grained, dull to slight sheen: → Slate (low grade; slaty cleavage; protolith = shale)
NO — fine-grained but with a visible silky sheen, crinkly surface: → Phyllite (low-intermediate grade; chlorite common)
YES — visible platy crystals, sparkling foliation: → Step 3
Step 3 (Foliated, visible crystals): What is the foliation style? ▼
Schistose foliation (wrinkly, platy mica flakes identifiable): → Schist. Identify specific index minerals (chlorite, biotite, garnet, staurolite, sillimanite) to determine grade. Name accordingly: Garnet-Mica Schist, etc.
Gneissic banding (alternating light and dark bands, coarse-grained): → Gneiss (high grade; light = quartz/feldspar, dark = biotite/hornblende)
Step 4 (Non-Foliated): What is the dominant mineral? ▼
Quartz (scratches glass easily, H=7, sugary texture, no foliation): → Quartzite; protolith = quartz sandstone
Calcite (fizzes with dilute HCl, white to gray, crystalline): → Marble; protolith = limestone or dolostone
Amphibole/Hornblende (dark, blade-like crystals, medium-coarse): → Amphibolite; protolith = basalt/gabbro
Dark, fine-grained, "baked" appearance, very hard, matte luster: → Hornfels; any protolith (contact metamorphism)
Conglomerate texture preserved, clasts fused: → Metaconglomerate; protolith = conglomerate

Metamorphic Grade

Metamorphic grade is the measure of the degree of metamorphism — the cumulative effect of temperature and pressure. It ranges from low grade (minimal change, protolith still recognizable) to high grade (extensive recrystallization, protolith unrecognizable).

Low Grade
~200–400°C
Intermediate Grade
~400–600°C
High Grade
>600°C
Important: Metamorphic grade can be determined from foliated rocks. Non-foliated rocks (quartzite, marble, hornfels) do not record grade in the same way — we typically note "N/A" or "not determined" for grade on non-foliated specimens.
